Meditation - HeadSpace

For the meditation component of the Self-care, Health, and Wellness journey project, I joined fellow 2019 Orientation Leaders Summer, Maya, Jimmy, Mykayla, and Amorie for a session on Landis Green.

Our guided meditation was facilitated by the free app, HeadSpace. In total, we spent about 20-30 minutes meditating, and about 45 minutes on Landis Green in total.

I have prior experience with meditation, so in this case, I did not have much trouble deciding on a topic to focus on. I decided to meditate on being motivated to finish the rest of the Spring 2019 semester strong. My previous semester was my most difficult, and disappointing ever, but now, I am doing better than I ever have in college, because I am determined to be eligible for the official Orientation Leader spot come summer. After Spring Break, I was initially feeling detached from school, and had lost sight of the very reason I had worked so hard in the first place. This meditation session was helpful for me because I reflected upon the reasons I wanted to do well, and helped me decide to re-focus on studying consistently, and managing my time well, so that I could continue to do well on assignments and tests.

That day was a specifically quiet day on Landis Green - there were no political messengers with loudspeakers, nor any tents from student organizations up. In fact, the ambience of Landis Green that day was extremely calm. This made it much easier to meditate. I am someone who typically meditates in the quiet, private space of my own room, so it being quiet outside was the main reason I was able to successfully meditate outside.

Counseling Center Event - Coffee Hour

I attended the International Coffee Hour, where RENEW was doing a tabling activity, where guests could have one-on-one conversations with members, and connect in a conversation about mental health.

The International Coffee Hour is an event held in the Globe every Friday, the main purpose of which is usually for guests to meet and converse with other students on campus. Oftentimes, it is hosted by an on-campus organization, and said organization may conduct an activity concurrently. In this case, guests were instructed to take a "Passport" card, and complete at least two activities, in order to receive a small prize.

The first activity I participated in was called "Guess the Language"; I was successfully able to guess which language 10 translations of a document detailing the purpose of the Counseling Center were written in. Though on the surface it was a simple quiz, its purpose was very clear to me - to not only demonstrate the fact that the Counseling Center is a resource that works very hard to ensure students have support for any mental circumstances they may face, but also that the organization is unassuming, and does not discriminate against students. Whether you speak another language, or no matter what situation you are going through, you can find support at the Counseling Center.

The second activity was called "Finding Connections," and involved stepping outside and having a two-minute conversation with two student members of RENEW. They pulled questions out of a cup, and used those as the basis of conversation. This questions were intended to provoke a deeper conversation about ways people can find support when they are not at their best mentally.

After completing the two activities, I was given a small stress ball, in the shape of a globe. By the end of the event, I felt very much reassured. I, like many other students, have gone through periods where I was struggling mentally, and had I been willing to seek help at the time, could have found outstanding support. I have confidence in the fact that I can visit the Counseling Center when counseling is what I need.

Reflection - Chabad Lubavitch

Though I did not physically attend, I researched the Chabad Lubavitch of Tallahassee, a Jewish ministry in the FSU InterFaith Council. The Chabad Lubavitch does a lot of outreach and programming, including "Friday Night Live," an event that targets FSU students, and aims to provide a space for them to interact with one another. The event is held weekly, and a Shabbat dinner is served. The Chabad Lubavitch is also active and vocal in Tallahassee, speaking at events like the recent inauguration of Governor Ron DeSantis.

Many students practice an organized religion, or are otherwise active spiritually, whether it is through meditation, or another practice. One's spirituality can be extremely beneficial to wellbeing, especially when it comes to matters of mental health. As someone who was raised Christian, I have seen firsthand how people who have gone through extreme adversity are able to motivate themselves to continue giving life a chance when they have faith in a benefactor watching over them. Regardless of one's beliefs, spirituality has the power to give people hope that nothing tangible is even remotely capable of doing. Besides just faith, most organized religions are communal activities, and religious student organizations provide avenues for students to meet others of the same faith, and to form long-lasting relationships with others who genuinely care about them. I myself have joined my local church's band, and that has been virtually my only musical involvement while in Tallahassee. Music is so important to me, and plays such an important role - being a member of my church has opened the door for me to do something I love, in the context of my faith.

Reflection - Resilience

Before completing the Student Resilience Project, I had a different idea of what resilience was. I believed that resilience was simply the quality of being able to approach difficult situations without showing visible signs of wear or frustration. Typically, when I thought of people who were resilient, I thought of famous figures, like Martin Luther King, Jr., or Maxine Waters, who faced adversity with sheer indignation and a refusal to take things passively. In my mind, this is what being resilient was all about - being a "trooper," and being strong enough to take any blow without conceding so much as even a scratch.

After completing the Student Resilience Project, however, I gained some new insight about another definition of resilience. Though I still feel that what it takes to be resilient is this ability to recognize situations that dissatisfy you, and take matters into your own hands to correct them, I no longer think that one has to deny and avoid their pain to be resilient. My previous definition could be applied to pain itself - part of being resilient is being able to look your pain square in the eye, accept it for what it is, and prioritize self-care, so that you can have strength again to fight another day.

It's really not as easy as I may have made it sound above, but I believe that the first step to being resilient is for me to at least come up with a definition and framework for what resilience even is, in the first place. With this new goal in mind, I had a clearer picture of ways I wanted to care better for myself.

Reflection - Hazing

I completed the Hazing - Test Your Knowledge challenge. I was expecting to get every question correct; however, I missed one question. I was unaware that hazing charges could result in a third degree for those convicted. Typical hazing stories involve things like underage drinking, staying up late, and wearing suits to class, which don't typically immediately jump out at you as things warranting a felony. However, the stories which I've heard in the news have resulted in outcomes like student death, or serious injury, so I can understand that things like that should be criminally punished, and agree.

I am not involved with or affiliated with any organizations that engage in hazing practices, but it is very clear to me how being hazed can affect a student, especially one who is new to college. Even things that might seem minor, like keeping pledges up late, can be detrimental to self-care. Without sleep, it is difficult to pay attention in class, to be productive, and to live a healthy lifestyle. This is a double-whammy punch, as it'll also be hard to find time to study and make up the time you missed.

Besides just school, being hazed can seriously affect one's health. Excessive drinking is a common hazing activity, which, for obvious reasons, is not healthy. Extreme drinking can lead to alcohol poisoning, or even death, and often causes people to do things they regret, which may even be criminal offenses.

Overall, hazing seriously throws the balance of a student's life off course, and makes it nearly impossible to conduct one's self in a healthy, responsible way. It is extremely important to know not only the dangers of hazing, but how to prevent it, and report it when it happens. At the end of the day, it is about keeping fellow students safe. One person can have a large impact, if they have the right tools available to them.

Self Care Kit

The first item I selected, and maybe the most important, was my OL water bottle. I have been working hard all semester long to get back into the shape I previously was in, and truly, it has been a hard grind. One difficulty I have found myself running into is that as school work progressively gets harder, and stress mounts, exercising regularly and eating healthy becomes less of a priority for me. However, I have been able to identify some habits I had developed that led to unhealthy eating, weight gain, and overall frustration with my progress, or lack thereof. The most important thing I can do is to drink water the moment I wake up. In the morning, I often feel hungrier than I actually am. Drinking even just a little bit of water fills me up a bit, and reminds me that I maybe don't need to eat more than what can fit on a single plate.

My second choice was a MIDI keyboard. I pride myself in being a person who loves music, and has the production know-how necessary to create good-sounding music worth releasing. However, for all the practicing I've ever done, the only music I've ever put on Spotify was a 4-song EP I released in high school. I've been telling myself for literally two years that I would put something out, but what really ends up happening is that I pack up my music gear, and shove it under my bed, while I sort the rest of my life together. Music itself is not my self-care goal, but my goal is to have my life to the point where I can comfortably do some sort of musical activity often, and maybe even release my record soon.

My third object, a pair of studio headphones, is also musical, but rather than serving as an eventual goal to attain, it is a reminder to myself that on stressful days, I can sit and listen to music to calm down. I have noticed that on the days where I feel the most drained, and the most irritable, I usually have not had any opportunity to listen to and sing along to music. I try hard to listen to music every day, and it is one of the few activities that helps me balance and center myself, and keep my emotions in order. This semester, I have had several days where I don't listen to music, and feel like the end of the world is imminent. So, I included the headphones, so that I can be sure to ground myself.

My fourth object, face wash, is not nearly as trivial as it might seem without context. I am a person who takes at least one shower every day; however, there are times, especially during stressful periods, where I slack on face and skin care. As someone who periodically has eczema flares, this can lead to prolonged frustration, as at times, I might encounter areas of dry skin that do not go away for a long time. This bottle of face wash is now sitting in my shower, so that I can be sure to use it every time I bathe.

My final object is a PC game controller, and serves to remind me to take some time out of my busy life to enjoy things. As any of my friends might tell you, I am truly a work-a-holic, and spend almost every waking hour doing some sort of project, or attempting to learn something new. Historically, it's been rare for me to specifically reserve leisure time. This semester, though, I bought a copy of NBA 2K19 for PC, and have been making time to slowly play it, and enjoy my weekends.

Reflection - Overall

Overall, this journey project was really and truly a journey. It reinforced for me the importance of taking care of myself, and beyond that, demonstrated to me that there are many different ways to take care of myself - where it be meditation, religious involvement, avoiding and preventing hazing, visiting the Counseling Center, or even making a self-care kit. Most of these things were not activities I regularly engaged - for example, I had never been to the University Counseling Center. Yet, it is important to note that I completed the entire journey project while on campus. There are many resources available to me to manage my health and well-being, they can all assist me with self-care, and they are all very accessible to me as an FSU student. As a Spring 2019 Orientation Leader, I can use what I have learned to help incoming students, and share my experiences with students who might go through some of the same challenges that I went through. Though this journey was about self-care, I am determined to take what I have learned, and use it to help others take better of their selves, and inspire them to share the message as well.
